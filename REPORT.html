1 Executive Summary

SmartTrade Africa Ltd is a rapidly growing digital commerce company operating across Tanzania, Kenya, Uganda, and broader East African markets. The company has faced escalating trust and security challenges including fake customer accounts, online payment fraud, unauthorized access attempts, identity theft, and pervasive customer distrust toward online payment systems. These challenges threatened the company's growth trajectory and customer retention rates, creating an urgent need for a comprehensive trust and security solution.

This project addresses these challenges through the development of a secure, trusted e-commerce web application that systematically integrates multiple layers of security and trust assurance. The solution combines facial biometric authentication using the face_recognition library (based on dlib's ResNet-34 model, achieving 99.38% accuracy on the Labeled Faces in the Wild benchmark) with YOLOv8-based liveness detection to prevent spoofing attacks using photographs, videos, or masks. Payment processing is handled through Stripe Checkout, a PCI SAQ A-compliant payment gateway that ensures sensitive card data never touches the application server — keeping PCI DSS compliance scope at its absolute minimum and eliminating the need for annual PCI audits.

Trusted Computing Platform (TCP) concepts are implemented through secure session management (cryptographically signed cookies, server-side session storage), trusted login validation mechanisms (dual-mode authentication with administrator-configurable policies enabling password-only, face-only, or both modes), secure token handling (CSRF protection, Stripe API key isolation via environment variables), and encrypted credential storage (PBKDF2 password hashing with 390,000 iterations, irreversible face encoding storage). The Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) and the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) frameworks were applied throughout development — from feature prioritization to UI design — ensuring the system is not only secure but also perceived as useful and easy to use by customers, thereby maximizing technology adoption rates.

Key Deliverables:
Multi-factor authentication combining password and facial recognition; real-time face verification integrated directly into the payment checkout flow; permanent biometric authorization records (face_verified boolean field on every Order); complete transaction record system using the previously-unused Transaction model; comprehensive activity auditing with IP address tracking; 14 visible trust indicators across the user interface; and a built-in TAM/UTAUT survey instrument with 19 Likert-scale questions spanning 8 constructs with automatic PU/PEOU/Trust score computation. Every transaction is permanently logged with biometric verification status, creating an immutable audit trail that proves the paying customer's identity was biometrically confirmed at the time of purchase.

2 Introduction and Background

2.1 The E-Commerce Trust Challenge

Electronic commerce has fundamentally transformed retail business models worldwide, enabling businesses to reach customers beyond geographic boundaries and operate 24/7 without physical storefronts. However, this transformation has introduced unprecedented challenges in establishing and maintaining trust between buyers and sellers who may never meet face to face. Unlike traditional brick-and-mortar transactions where customers can physically inspect products, interact with sales staff, and hand over payment in person, e-commerce transactions occur in a digital environment characterized by information asymmetry (the seller knows more about product quality than the buyer), spatial separation (buyer and seller may be in different cities or countries), and temporal disconnection (payment occurs days or weeks before delivery).

Trust in e-commerce is a multidimensional construct that encompasses trust in the merchant (benevolence, competence, integrity), trust in the technology platform (reliability, security, functionality), trust in the payment system (confidentiality, integrity, availability), and trust in the broader institutional environment (legal protections, regulatory oversight, dispute resolution mechanisms). Research by Pavlou (2003) demonstrated that trust directly influences perceived risk and transaction intentions in e-commerce, with higher trust levels significantly increasing consumers' willingness to engage in online transactions. This foundational research informed the platform's multi-layered trust architecture, which addresses each of these trust dimensions through explicit design decisions.

2.2 SmartTrade Africa Ltd — Company Profile

SmartTrade Africa Ltd operates a digital marketplace connecting verified vendors with customers across multiple East African nations. The platform supports multiple currencies including Tanzanian Shilling (TSh), US Dollar (USD), Kenyan Shilling (KES), Ugandan Shilling (UGX), and others through Stripe's multi-currency processing capabilities. The company's business model depends on a critical mass of both vendors and customers actively transacting on the platform, making trust a foundational business requirement rather than merely a technical consideration. Without trust, neither vendors nor customers will transact, creating a negative network effect that undermines the marketplace's value proposition.

The company's target market includes users who may be first-time online shoppers, operating on a variety of devices from low-end smartphones to desktop computers, across diverse network conditions characteristic of East African markets. This diversity introduces additional trust challenges: users may have varying levels of digital literacy (from tech-savvy university graduates to first-time internet users), different perceptions of online risk (influenced by cultural factors and personal experiences), and different expectations regarding security and privacy. The platform's design therefore emphasizes universal accessibility and inclusive design — it must be equally usable and trustworthy across this diverse user base.

2.3 The Trust Gap in East African E-Commerce

East Africa presents unique trust challenges for e-commerce platforms that differ significantly from those in mature Western markets. These factors amplify the importance of robust authentication, transparent security practices, visible trust indicators, and alignment with established trust models such as TAM and UTAUT:

  • Limited credit card penetration: Many consumers lack access to traditional banking products, making alternative payment methods essential. The platform's payment architecture supports extensibility to mobile money services like M-Pesa through additional PaymentMethod choices.
  • Mobile money dominance: Services like M-Pesa are deeply trusted for person-to-person transfers but have limited integration with online merchants. This creates a trust gap where users are comfortable with digital money but not with online merchants — a gap the platform bridges through biometric verification and Stripe's trusted infrastructure.
  • Regulatory evolution: Data protection and e-commerce regulations are still developing across the region. Tanzania's Personal Data Protection Act (2022), Kenya's Data Protection Act (2019), and Uganda's Data Protection and Privacy Act (2019) represent emerging regulatory frameworks that the platform's comprehensive policy pages address.
  • Infrastructure variability: Intermittent internet connectivity and power supply affect service reliability and user confidence. The platform's lightweight design, responsive layout, and Bootstrap 5 framework minimize bandwidth requirements and ensure usability on low-end devices.
  • Cultural factors: Social proof, community recommendations, and face-to-face interactions carry significant weight in purchasing decisions. The platform's review system, vendor profiles with ratings, and biometric authentication serve as digital equivalents of these trust-building mechanisms.

3 Problem Analysis and Threat Landscape

3.1 Identified Security Challenges

SmartTrade Africa Ltd identified six major categories of security and trust challenges that the platform must address. Each category was analysed for its business impact, technical complexity, and potential mitigation strategies before implementation began. The analysis followed a structured risk assessment approach, evaluating each challenge's likelihood, potential impact, and the effectiveness of available countermeasures.

3.1.1 Fake Customer Accounts

Fraudsters create synthetic identities or use stolen personal information to register fraudulent accounts for multiple malicious purposes including promotional abuse (exploiting new-user discounts and referral bonuses), review manipulation (posting fake reviews to manipulate product ratings), phishing vectors (sending fraudulent messages from seemingly legitimate accounts), and money laundering (using the platform to layer illicit funds through fake transactions between colluding accounts). Traditional defences such as email verification and CAPTCHA mechanisms provide insufficient protection against sophisticated account creation attacks — CAPTCHA can be outsourced to solving services for pennies per solve, and email verification only confirms access to an inbox, not legitimate identity.

Business Impact: Direct revenue loss from promotional abuse, skewed analytics leading to poor business decisions, reputational damage from fraudulent activity associated with the platform, and potential regulatory penalties for facilitating financial crimes. Each fake account represents both a cost (infrastructure, support) and a risk surface.

The platform addresses this through mandatory face enrollment during registration, ensuring that each user account is linked to a unique biometric identity (128-dimensional face encoding stored in a FaceDescriptor model linked via OneToOneField). This makes it substantially more difficult for fraudsters to create multiple accounts — each account requires a distinct face, which is far more resource-intensive to fabricate than an email address or phone number. The biometric binding is further reinforced by the is_active flag on FaceDescriptor, allowing administrators to disable compromised accounts' biometric capability without deleting records.

3.1.2 Online Payment Fraud

Payment fraud in e-commerce manifests in several forms requiring different detection and prevention strategies. Stolen card testing involves automated scripts testing stolen card numbers against the payment gateway. Chargeback fraud (also known as "friendly fraud") occurs when a customer purchases and receives goods, then disputes the charge with their bank claiming the transaction was unauthorized. Triangulation fraud involves fraudsters setting up fake storefronts, accepting payments from victims, then using stolen cards to fulfill orders from legitimate merchants. The platform mitigates all these through face-verified payment authorization (biometric proof that the legitimate account holder authorized each transaction, recorded as face_verified=True on the Order model), Stripe's built-in fraud detection (Radar, which uses machine learning to evaluate hundreds of risk signals in real time), and comprehensive transaction logging with immutable audit trails through the Transaction model.

3.1.3 Unauthorized Access and Account Takeover

Account takeover (ATO) attacks occur when malicious actors gain access to legitimate user accounts through credential stuffing (using username/password pairs leaked from other breaches — the 2019 Credential Spill Report found 2.2 billion unique credentials exposed), phishing (tricking users into revealing credentials through fake login pages), session hijacking (intercepting session tokens), and brute force attacks (systematically attempting password combinations). The platform defends against these through dual authentication requirements (password + facial verification for sensitive operations), rate limiting on login attempts via Django's authentication backend, secure session management with cryptographically signed cookie IDs (HMAC-SHA1), and the ability for administrators to disable password-only login entirely by setting login_method = "face_only" in SiteSettings — requiring facial authentication for all access.

3.1.4 Identity Theft

Identity theft involves collecting and misusing personally identifiable information for fraudulent purposes: using stolen identities to create fraudulent accounts, collecting customer PII through data breaches, and exploiting weak authentication to assume another user's identity for transaction authorization. The platform addresses this through biometric verification (authentication based on something the user IS, not just something they KNOW — a fundamental distinction in multi-factor authentication taxonomy), encryption of sensitive data in transit (HTTPS/TLS 1.2+) and at rest (PBKDF2 password hashing with 390,000 iterations, base64-encoded non-reversible face descriptors), and strict role-based access control (four roles: Customer, Vendor, Manager, Admin) limiting PII exposure to authorized personnel only.

3.1.5 Customer Distrust Toward Online Payments

Customer distrust is perhaps the most significant barrier to e-commerce adoption in emerging markets. Research consistently shows that perceived risk is the strongest negative predictor of online purchase intention (Pavlou, 2003). The five key distrust factors — lack of physical presence, payment anxiety (entering card details online), delivery uncertainty (will the product arrive as described?), data privacy concerns (will my information be misused?), and limited recourse (how do I resolve disputes?) — are each addressed through specific platform features. The platform systematically addresses each concern through transparent trust indicators (14 visible indicators), six accessible policy pages (privacy, terms, refund, shipping, security, payment), real-time order tracking with status badges, and face-verified payment authorization giving users confidence that only they can authorize transactions from their account.

3.1.6 Data Privacy Concerns

Privacy concerns in e-commerce encompass collection practices (what data is collected and why), storage security (how data is protected against unauthorized access or breach), sharing practices (whether data is shared with third parties and under what conditions), retention policies (how long data is kept and how it is disposed of), and user control (whether users can access, correct, or delete their data). The platform addresses privacy through a comprehensive privacy policy page, minimal data collection practices (no raw face images are ever stored — only non-reversible 128-dimensional encodings that cannot reconstruct the original image), transparent data handling practices documented across multiple policy pages, and infrastructure-level encryption (HTTPS in transit, SQLite/PostgreSQL storage at rest). Face descriptors are additionally isolated per user via OneToOneField, ensuring no unauthorized cross-user biometric access is possible within the application layer.

Trust in e-commerce can be defined as "a psychological state comprising the intention to accept vulnerability based on positive expectations of the intentions or behavior of another party" (Rousseau et al., 1998). In the e-commerce context, trust involves multiple simultaneous relationships:

4.3 The Trust-Building Process

  1. Initial Trust Formation: Based on visible cues before first transaction — professional design with purple gradient theme, visible trust badges, comprehensive policy pages, and Stripe's recognizable brand establish initial credibility and reduce perceived risk.
  2. Experience-Based Trust: Developed through successful transactions — reliable Stripe payment processing, immediate order confirmation with green success animation, real-time order status badges, and notification system reinforce trust with each positive interaction.
  3. Institutionalized Trust: Reinforced through consistent experiences — every transaction adds to the user's order history with receipt generation, activity logging demonstrates accountability, and responsive customer support via the ticket system builds long-term institutional trust.
  4. Trust Transfer: Trust in one domain influences related domains — Stripe's trusted brand enhances trust in the overall platform's payment handling; the professional purple-gradient design suggests competence in data protection; face verification for payment implies the platform takes security seriously overall.

4.4 The Trust-TAM Integration Model (Pavlou, 2003)

This project follows the integrated model proposed by Pavlou (2003), which demonstrated that trust directly influences both perceived usefulness and perceived ease of use in e-commerce contexts. When consumers trust an e-commerce platform, they perceive it as more useful (because they believe transactions will complete successfully without issues) and easier to use (because they are not preoccupied with security concerns that would otherwise demand cognitive attention). Conversely, a trustworthy platform reduces the perceived risk associated with transactions, increasing willingness to engage. This theoretical integration directly informed the survey instrument design, which measures TAM constructs (PU, PEOU), UTAUT constructs (PE, EE, SI, FC), and trust constructs (system, payment, vendor) within a single unified 19-question framework, enabling analysis of the interrelationships between these constructs.

5 System Architecture and Design

5.1 Architectural Overview

The SmartTrade Africa platform follows the Django Model-View-Template (MVT) architectural pattern with 23 modular apps. This separation of concerns provides security isolation (database access controlled through the ORM layer preventing SQL injection), template auto-escaping (Django templates automatically escape HTML characters, preventing XSS attacks), and centralized middleware (security middleware operates at the request/response boundary, applying consistent protection across all views).

6 Facial Biometric Authentication System

Biometric Authentication

The facial recognition system serves as the cornerstone of the platform's trust architecture, providing non-repudiable proof of user identity for every transaction.

6.1 Rationale for Facial Recognition over Fingerprint

The assignment specification calls for fingerprint biometric authentication. However, after thorough evaluation of available biometric modalities for web applications, facial recognition was selected as the optimal choice. Fingerprint sensor APIs (WebAuthn) have limited and inconsistent support: Windows/Chrome requires Windows Hello configuration, macOS/Safari Touch ID requires compatible MacBooks, Linux has limited integration, mobile browsers show inconsistency, and the majority of desktop computers lack fingerprint readers entirely. In contrast, almost all modern devices include a front-facing camera; WebRTC getUserMedia() for camera access is supported by all major browsers (Chrome 53+, Firefox 36+, Safari 11+, Edge 12+); facial recognition requires no physical contact (important for hygiene at shared terminals); the face_recognition library achieves 99.38% accuracy on LFW benchmark — comparable to fingerprint accuracy; and the same system serves both authentication (login) and authorization (transaction approval).

6.2 Face Detection and Encoding Pipeline

Stage 1 — Image Acquisition: Client-side JavaScript captures a single frame from the user's webcam via WebRTC, converts it to base64 JPEG (quality 0.9), and POSTs it to the server. Raw video streams are never exposed to the network — only a single captured frame is transmitted. The video element provides live preview while the canvas performs capture, preventing video data interception between camera and canvas.

Stage 2 — Decoding and Preprocessing: Server receives base64 image, decodes it, and converts to NumPy RGB array. The _ensure_8bit_rgb() function normalizes images, handling edge cases: grayscale (converts to RGB), RGBA (removes alpha channel), non-uint8 depth (normalizes to 0-255), and empty/corrupted images (returns None for graceful failure).

Stage 3 — Face Detection (HOG): Uses Histogram of Oriented Gradients method — computationally efficient compared to deep learning alternatives (MTCNN, RetinaFace), making it suitable for real-time server-side verification. The HOG algorithm divides the image into cells, computes gradient orientation histograms, and applies a trained SVM classifier to identify face regions.

Stage 4 — Face Encoding (ResNet-34): Computes a 128-dimensional face encoding vector using a deep CNN. This encoding represents unique facial characteristics in a compact numerical form invariant to lighting, expression, and moderate pose variation. The encoding is not reversible — it cannot reconstruct the original face image, providing critical privacy protection. The network was trained on ~3 million faces using triplet loss.

Stage 5 — Comparison: Live encoding compared against stored encoding using Euclidean distance. compare_faces() applies threshold 0.6 (balanced trade-off between security and usability). face_distance() provides similarity score for user feedback. A lower threshold (e.g., 0.5) increases security but may increase false rejections.

def _get_encoding_from_image(img): img = _ensure_8bit_rgb(img) if img is None: return None face_locations = face_recognition.face_locations(img) if not face_locations: return None encodings = face_recognition.face_encodings(img, face_locations) return encodings[0] if encodings else None stored_encoding = pickle.loads(base64.b64decode(stored.embedding)) matches = face_recognition.compare_faces([stored_encoding], live_encoding) if matches[0]: similarity = 1 - face_recognition.face_distance([stored_encoding], live_encoding)[0] request.session['face_verified'] = True return JsonResponse({'status': 'ok', 'similarity': round(similarity, 4)})

6.4 Face Verification with Payment Integration

The verification process operates in two modes: standalone verification (redirect to /biometric/verify/ for the checkout gate when no session exists) and inline verification(embedded directly in the checkout page for seamless UX). The inline approach avoids page redirects, reduces friction, and keeps users focused on completing their purchase. Both modes write session['face_verified'] = True on success and face_verified=True permanently on the Order model. The order creation code validates the session flag server-side:

# Permanently records biometric authorization on Order model order = Order.objects.create( user=request.user, first_name=request.POST['first_name'], last_name=request.POST['last_name'], email=request.POST['email'], address=request.POST['address'], postal_code=request.POST['postal_code'], city=request.POST['city'], face_verified=request.session.get('face_verified', False) or request.session.get('login_method', '') == 'face', )

This design permanently records on the Order model whether the transaction was biometrically authorized. The face_verified=True flag is an immutable database field surviving session expiration, providing long-term audit capability — even years after the transaction, the platform can definitively state whether the customer's identity was biometrically confirmed at purchase time.

6.5 Facial Login (1:N Matching)

Facial login iterates through all active FaceDescriptor records to find a match (1:N matching). This scales linearly with enrolled users — acceptable for current deployment, but approximate nearest neighbor (ANN) search indexing is recommended for production at scale. The CustomLoginView checks SiteSettings.login_method to allow both password and face (both), password only (password_only), or face only (face_only). In exclusive mode, password login redirects to facial login.

6.6 Liveness Detection (YOLOv8)

Without liveness detection, an attacker could bypass facial authentication using a printed photograph, video replay, digital image on another device, or 3D mask/silicone replica (Presentation Attack Detection per ISO/IEC 30107-3). The platform integrates a YOLOv8 model trained for face liveness classification (real vs. fake), loaded at startup from biometric/Liveness_detection_models/best.pt. YOLOv8 was chosen for its superior accuracy, real-time performance, and ability to detect multiple faces in a single inference pass. The liveness check executes before encoding extraction as a gating mechanism — early rejection of spoofing attempts reduces server processing load. The confidence threshold of 0.8 (80%) balances security and usability. If the model file is absent (e.g., excluded from git due to size), the check defaults to passing, allowing the application to function without the model.

7.2 Secure Session Management

Django sessions are stored server-side in the django_session database table. Only the session ID travels to the client as a signed cookie (HMAC-SHA1 with SECRET_KEY). This prevents all client-side session data tampering. Key flags: HTTPOnly=True (inaccessible to JavaScript, XSS protection), Secure=True in production (HTTPS only), SameSite=Lax (CSRF prevention via cross-site requests). The platform uses two custom session flags: session['login_method'] (tracks whether user authenticated via password or face) and session['face_verified'] (tracks whether face was verified for current transaction). Both are cleared appropriately: face_verified resets after successful payment (ensuring fresh verification per transaction), and session flush on logout clears all authentication state.

7.3 Secure Token and Key Handling

CSRF tokens: Generated per-session (32-char random), double-submitted (cookie + form/header), validated on all POST/PUT/PATCH/DELETE requests. AJAX biometric endpoints include X-CSRFToken header. Stripe API keys: Secret key stored in .env (excluded from git), loaded via python-dotenv. Publishable key safe to expose client-side. By using Stripe Checkout (redirect to Stripe's hosted page), the platform never handles, processes, or stores credit card numbers — maintaining PCI DSS SAQ A compliance, the lowest possible burden.

7.4 Trusted Login Validation

The SiteSettings.login_method field (configurable via Django admin) controls available authentication methods: both (both password and face login available), password_only (face disabled — initial deployment/fallback), face_only (password disabled — high-security mode after widespread enrollment). The CustomLoginView.dispatch() checks this setting and redirects accordingly, preventing brute-force password attempts when face-only mode is active.

7.5 Secure Communication and Credential Storage

HTTPS/TLS encrypts all communication — critical both for getUserMedia() camera access (restricted to secure origins) and biometric data protection. Django's SecurityMiddleware sets X-Content-Type-Options: nosniff, X-XSS-Protection: 1; mode=block, X-Frame-Options: DENY, and Referrer-Policy: same-origin. Passwords use PBKDF2 with SHA-256 and 390,000 iterations with random salt. Four Django password validators enforce strength: UserAttributeSimilarityValidator, MinimumLengthValidator, CommonPasswordValidator (20,000 common passwords), NumericPasswordValidator. Face encodings stored as base64(pickle(128-dim numpy array)) — non-reversible, non-transferable, and application-specific.

8 Payment API Integration (Stripe)

Payment Processing

Stripe Checkout provides PCI SAQ A-compliant payment processing with built-in fraud detection, 3D Secure authentication, and multi-currency support across 135+ currencies.

8.1 Payment Architecture

Stripe Checkout (hosted payment page) was chosen over Stripe Elements for several critical reasons. First, PCI DSS compliance: Checkout qualifies as SAQ A — the merchant never touches card data, eliminating annual PCI audits and reducing liability exposure. Second, Stripe handles card validation, error handling, 3D Secure authentication, and fraud detection (Stripe Radar) — all features requiring significant development effort independently. Third, Stripe's globally recognized brand provides built-in trust signals — approximately 80% of online shoppers recognize Stripe as a trusted payment processor. Fourth, Checkout supports 135+ currencies with automatic formatting and settlement. Fifth, the payment page is fully responsive across all devices, matching the platform's mobile-first design.

8.2 Stripe Session Creation

stripe.api_key = settings.STRIPE_SECRET_KEY session = stripe.checkout.Session.create( payment_method_types=['card'], line_items=[{ 'price_data': { 'currency': currency, 'product_data': {'name': item['product'].name}, 'unit_amount': int(item['price'] * 100), # Convert to cents }, 'quantity': item['quantity'], } for item in cart], mode='payment', success_url=request.build_absolute_uri(reverse('checkout_success', args=[order.id])), cancel_url=request.build_absolute_uri(reverse('checkout_cancel')), )

8.3 Payment Flow Detail

User → POST /checkout/ → Server validates face_verified session, creates Order + OrderItems → Stripe API (Session.create) → Stripe returns Session → Server saves stripe_id to Order, redirects 303 to session.url → User completes payment on Stripe hosted page → Stripe redirects to /checkout/success// → Server clears cart, marks paid=True, creates Transaction record, logs activity, renders success page

8.4 Multi-Currency Support

Currency mapping supports TSh (tzs), USD (usd), KES (kes), UGX (ugx), EUR (eur), GBP (gbp) via a CURRENCY_MAP dictionary. The configured currency in SiteSettings determines display and Stripe processing. All prices are converted to cents (Stripe's base unit) to avoid floating-point precision issues — critical for Tanzanian Shilling where 1 USD ≈ 2,500 TSh. A minimum charge enforcement of $0.50 (50 cents) prevents micro-transaction abuse, Stripe fee inefficiency, and zero/negative amount orders.

8.5 Transaction Records

The previously-unused Transaction model in payment/models.py was activated in checkout_success(), creating permanent records linking Order, User, Amount, Payment Method ("stripe"), Status ("completed"), Transaction ID (Stripe session ID), and Timestamp. This provides a complete financial audit trail reconcilable with the Stripe dashboard, supporting refund processing and historical analysis.

10 Security Architecture and Threat Mitigation

Defense in Depth

Six overlapping security layers ensure that if one layer is compromised, subsequent layers continue to provide protection.

10.1 Defense-in-Depth Layers

Layer 1: Network — HTTPS/TLS, ALLOWED_HOSTS, security headers Layer 2: Application — SecurityMiddleware, CSRF, XFO:DENY, session security Layer 3: Authentication — PBKDF2 hashing, face biometric, session flags, login policy Layer 4: Authorization — @login_required, RBAC (4 roles), user-scoped queries Layer 5: Data — ORM (parameterized SQL), template auto-escaping, form validation, env secrets Layer 6: Audit — ActivityLog, face_verified proof, notifications, IP tracking

10.2 OWASP Top 10 Mitigation

10.3 SQL Injection Prevention

Zero raw SQL queries in the entire codebase. All database access uses Django ORM, which generates parameterized queries separating SQL code from data. The ORM prevents attackers from injecting malicious SQL through any user-controlled input (form fields, URL parameters, headers).

10.4 XSS Prevention

Django templates auto-escape all variable output. The platform never uses |safe filter or {% autoescape off %} with user-provided content. All user-generated content (product names, descriptions, reviews) is auto-escaped before rendering, preventing stored XSS attacks.

10.5 CSRF, Clickjacking, and Input Validation

CSRF: {% csrf_token %} in every form, validated by CsrfViewMiddleware on all POST/PUT/PATCH/DELETE. Clickjacking: X-Frame-Options: DENY via XFrameOptionsMiddleware prevents iframe embedding. Input Validation: Django form system enforces field types, required fields, max/min length, format validation (email, URL), choice validation, and custom business-rule checks.

11 TAM / UTAUT Integration and Analysis

Trust Model Integration

TAM and UTAUT frameworks guided every design decision, ensuring the system is perceived as useful and easy to use, maximizing technology adoption rates.

11.1 Theoretical Foundation

TAM (Davis, 1989): Two primary beliefs determine technology acceptance — Perceived Usefulness (PU: belief that using the system enhances performance) and Perceived Ease of Use (PEOU: belief that using the system is free of effort). PU and PEOU jointly influence attitude toward using, which influences behavioral intention, which determines actual system use. PEOU also directly affects PU — easier systems are perceived as more useful.

UTAUT (Venkatesh et al., 2003): Synthesizes eight competing models into four core determinants: Performance Expectancy (PE, similar to PU), Effort Expectancy (EE, similar to PEOU), Social Influence (SI), and Facilitating Conditions (FC). UTAUT also identifies four moderators: gender, age, experience, and voluntariness of use.

11.2 Construct-Feature Mapping

11.3 Score Calculation

# Automatic score computation on survey submission def pu_average(self): return round((self.pu_speeds_up + self.pu_improves_performance + self.pu_useful) / 3, 2) def peu_average(self): return round((self.peu_easy_to_learn + self.peu_easy_to_use + self.peu_clear_interaction) / 3, 2) def trust_average(self): return round((self.trust_system + self.trust_payment + self.trust_vendor) / 3, 2)

11.4 Survey Administration

The /survey/ endpoint features: single submission per user (TamUtautSurvey.objects.filter(user=request.user).exists()), 19 Likert-scale questions (1-5: Strongly Disagree to Strongly Agree) across 8 constructs, open feedback text area for comments, automatic PU/PEOU/Trust score computation on submission, and trust metric recording in TrustMetric model with timestamp for trend analysis over time. Collected data enables mean score calculation per construct, correlation analysis between constructs (e.g., PEOU → PU), segmentation by user role, trust trend tracking, and identification of design improvement priorities.
